building real software with AI

a framework for people who've never used AI to build software — based on how real teams ship real products to real customers.

chapter 1
the mindset shift
you're not using a tool — you're running a team

the biggest mistake new users make is treating AI like a search engine. type a question, get an answer, done.

the teams that actually ship software with AI treat it differently. they treat it like hiring a brilliant junior engineer who has amnesia between shifts.

this engineer shows up every day ready to work. they know every programming language. they can read any codebase in seconds. but they don't remember what you told them yesterday unless you wrote it down somewhere they can find it.

mental model

the orchestra conductor

building software with AI is like conducting an orchestra. you don't play every instrument — you couldn't, and you don't need to. but you choose the piece (what to build), set the tempo (when to ship), bring in the right sections at the right time (which capabilities to invoke), listen for wrong notes (verify the output), and keep everyone on the same page (documentation). the musicians are world-class. your job is to make sure they're playing the same song.

your actual job

your job isn't to write code. it's to be the person who:

  1. knows what to build — product direction
  2. knows what matters — business context
  3. knows what's fragile — production awareness
  4. verifies what actually happened — quality control

the AI's job is everything else: writing the code, running the commands, debugging the errors, deploying the changes.

from the field

a startup team needed to migrate their task management system from a local server to a shared cloud server, adding multi-user support along the way. the human never wrote a line of code. they described what they wanted ("port the whole thing over to the cloud server"), provided context the AI couldn't infer ("my teammate doesn't have a local copy of the repo"), and caught gaps the AI missed ("does it search DMs too?"). the migration shipped in one session.

the human's contribution wasn't code — it was direction, context, and quality control.

check your understanding
what's the primary role of the human when building software with AI?
the most effective humans focus on what the AI can't do: deciding what to build, providing business context, catching things the AI can't see, and verifying the actual results. implementation is what the AI does best.
chapter 2
the five things only you can do
what you bring to the collaboration that AI cannot replace

1. direction

the AI can build anything. it cannot decide what to build. that's you.

mental model

the restaurant kitchen

you're the head chef deciding the menu. the AI is the entire kitchen staff. they can execute any recipe brilliantly, but if you say "make something good," you'll get something random. if you say "make a mushroom risotto, the rice is in the third pantry, and the customer is lactose intolerant," you'll get exactly what you need.

2. context

the AI can read every file in your codebase. what it can't see: who your customers are, which systems are fragile, what your team tried last month that didn't work, why that weird workaround exists, or that the CEO is demoing this to investors on thursday.

3. judgment

the AI will confidently present guesses as conclusions. it might tell you "the database is rate-limiting" when someone actually unplugged the ethernet cable.

mental model

the GPS

AI is like a GPS that's usually right but sometimes confidently drives you into a lake. your job is to look out the window. when the AI says "the issue is X," ask: "how confident are you?" (forces honesty), "why X but not Y?" (tests the logic), and "what would prove you wrong?" (reveals blind spots).

4. risk awareness

the AI optimizes for completing the task. you optimize for not destroying what's already working. you're the one who says "what happens to the 47 existing bookings when we make this change?"

5. verification

the most dangerous phrase in AI-assisted development: "it looks like it worked."

from the field

a team's data scraper ran for 3 straight days, reporting success each time. zero records collected. the API had silently returned empty results when credits ran out — no error, no warning, just "success" with nothing inside. a background process "started successfully" but crashed 1 second after launch. a calendar integration created events at the wrong time because the AI confused PST and PDT.

verification means checking the actual output, not just the exit code. query the database. open the calendar. load the page on your phone.

check your understanding
a data pipeline reports "completed successfully" three days in a row. what should you do?
"success" only means the process didn't crash — it doesn't guarantee the output is correct. in the real-world example, the API returned HTTP 200 with empty data when credits ran out. always verify the actual results, not just the status code.
chapter 3
how specificity changes everything
the quality of your input determines the quality of AI's output

this is the single highest-leverage skill you can develop. the difference between a vague request and a specific one is the difference between a 20-minute rabbit hole and a correct answer on the first try.

vague

"fix the login"

specific

"users on mobile Safari get a white screen after entering their OTP. console shows a CORS error. started after yesterday's deploy."

prescriptive

"create a React component with useState hooks and a Postgres table"

outcome-focused

"we need to notify readers when content they've already read gets updated, without spoiling parts they haven't reached yet"

the pattern

describe outcomes, not implementations. let the AI propose the architecture, then steer it. you say "what" — the AI figures out "how."

but when something specific matters to you — "use console logging, PM2 logs are terrible" — correct immediately. this is the right ratio: delegate implementation details, own the decisions that matter.

mental model

the architect and the builder

you don't tell the builder which nails to use. you tell them you want a door that locks, a window that faces south, and a floor that holds a piano. you describe the requirements — they choose the materials and methods. but if you know the soil is clay and the builder doesn't, you speak up.

front-loading requirements

every requirement discovered after building starts costs a round trip: redo, redeploy, retest. a team found this the hard way when they didn't mention "it needs to work in DMs" until after the feature was built and deployed. one sentence upfront would have saved three commit-push-deploy cycles.

you don't need to spec everything perfectly. but if you have a mental model of the full feature, dump it as a rough bullet list before the AI starts building.

check your understanding
you want the AI to build a notification system. which request will get the best result?
the best request describes the outcome (what users experience), provides constraints (batching, user count), and lets the AI choose the implementation. too vague = wrong direction. too prescriptive = missed opportunities for better architecture.
chapter 4
the three layers of AI memory
how to stop re-explaining everything every conversation

out of the box, AI forgets everything between conversations. that's fine for asking questions. it's useless for building software over weeks and months.

production teams solve this with three layers of persistent context.

layer 1: the rules file — "how we do things here"

a file the AI reads at the start of every conversation. short, opinionated, non-negotiable. your team's constitution.

mental model

the employee handbook

every company has norms. don't force push to main. always add logging. commit after every change. don't change the approach without asking first. you wouldn't re-explain these to a returning employee every morning — you'd point them to the handbook. the rules file is that handbook for AI.

layer 2: skills — "here's everything about X"

documents that give AI specialist-level knowledge about specific domains. when you mention "deploy" or "database issue," the relevant skill loads automatically — complete with server addresses, commands, API keys, and step-by-step procedures.

mental model

the specialist's brain

when you visit a cardiologist, you don't explain what a heart is. the specialist already knows the anatomy, common problems, and standard treatments. they just need YOUR symptoms. skills give the AI specialist-level knowledge so you can skip straight to your problem.

layer 3: memory — "what we learned last time"

after you discover that a certain API silently fails on specific inputs, or that a deploy script overwrites changes unless you push to git first, you write it down. memory stores these learnings so the AI doesn't make the same mistake twice.

from the field

a team kept their AI configuration in a shared git repository. when one team member improved a skill document (adding a missing deployment step that had caused an outage), the other team member got the fix on their next git pull. every session after that, the AI automatically knew the correct deployment procedure. one 5-minute documentation update prevented a class of errors permanently.

documentation for the AI pays back more than code. a 30-minute skill doc saves 15 minutes per conversation, forever.

check your understanding
you've explained your deployment process to the AI three times across three conversations. what should you do?
if you're saying the same thing every conversation, it belongs in persistent context. a skill document or rules file is read automatically, eliminates the re-explanation, and ensures consistency. the time investment pays back across every future session.
chapter 5
the workflow
orient → plan → build → verify → document

phase 1: orient

state what you want to accomplish. be specific. the AI automatically loads your rules, relevant skills, and memory — so it already knows your infrastructure, standards, and who you are.

phase 2: plan

for anything beyond a simple fix, the AI proposes a plan. you review it — not for code quality, but for missing requirements, edge cases, and things the AI doesn't know about your system.

mental model

the blueprint review

you're the building owner reviewing the architect's blueprints. you don't need to know how to calculate load-bearing requirements — but you do need to say "there's no bathroom on this floor" and "the front door opens into a wall."

phase 3: build

the AI writes code, creates files, runs commands, deploys. you don't need to watch every keystroke — check in at milestones, not after every line.

stay available for questions. provide context the AI can't infer. correct mistakes early — it's cheaper to fix direction than to rebuild later.

phase 4: verify

this is where most people drop the ball. the AI says "done" and you say "great." three days later a customer reports a bug.

test the feature on a real device. check the database. verify the deployment. test the edge cases: no data, 10,000 rows, mobile, slow network.

phase 5: document

update your skill docs and memory with what you learned. the AI helped you discover that a certain config flag needs to be set — write that down so neither of you discovers it again.

from the field

a team was migrating off a third-party calendar integration. they followed the workflow precisely: oriented (identified the problem — credentials getting revoked), planned (proposed a phased migration with a "mirror mode" for testing), built (replaced the integration piece by piece), verified (manually checked the google calendar for correct events, caught a PST/PDT time bug), and documented (updated the infrastructure skill so the next conversation started with accurate context). the migration shipped cleanly because every phase got its due attention.

the verify phase caught a time zone bug that no test would have found. the document phase prevented the next developer from reading stale documentation.

check your understanding
the AI just finished building a booking system and says "deployment complete." what's the most important next step?
verification comes before documentation. test the critical path with real data on a real device — make a booking, check the database, verify the calendar event, confirm the email sent. the gap between "code looks right" and "it works end-to-end" is where most bugs live.
chapter 6
debugging with AI
data first, theories second — and always push back

debugging is where AI collaboration shines brightest — and where it can go most wrong. the AI can investigate logs, query databases, trace code paths, and propose fixes faster than any human. but it can also confidently lead you down the wrong path for 45 minutes.

rule 1: data before theories

don't let the AI theorize about what might be wrong. have it look at what IS wrong. check the logs first. query the database. hit the health endpoint. once you have data, theories become much more targeted.

rule 2: push back on confidence

the AI will present hypotheses as conclusions. "the issue is probably X" sounds authoritative. it might be wrong.

from the field

when google calendar credentials kept getting revoked, the AI suggested the cause was "aggressive API polling rate-limiting the tokens." the human asked: "how confident are you?" — the AI admitted it was an educated guess. the human then asked: "why would it target one user and not the other 79?" — a simple question that exposed the theory didn't hold up. the actual cause turned out to be their own monitoring script accidentally disconnecting credentials.

two questions — "how confident?" and "why this and not that?" — saved hours of debugging the wrong thing.

rule 3: give AI access to the real system

teams that give AI SSH access to their servers, database access, and API credentials debug 3x faster. instead of "run this command, paste the output, wait for analysis, repeat," the AI can directly check logs, query tables, and verify endpoints.

rule 4: screenshots are underrated

when something looks wrong in the UI, screenshot it. the AI can read screenshots. "it shows no data" + screenshot is 10x more useful than "it's not working" because the AI can see the URL, the page state, error messages, and UI context simultaneously.

check your understanding
the AI says "the 500 error is probably caused by a database connection timeout." what's the best response?
before accepting any theory, ask for evidence. "probably" means the AI is guessing. checking the actual error log takes 10 seconds and tells you exactly what happened — it might be a timeout, or it might be a completely different issue. data before theories.
chapter 7
the traps everyone falls into
mistakes observed across real teams — and how to avoid them

trap 1: being too vague

"fix the login" → AI investigates 15 possible issues. "mobile Safari shows white screen after OTP entry, CORS error in console, started after yesterday's deploy" → AI goes straight to the answer.

include: what you expected, what happened, when it started, what changed recently.

trap 2: not pushing back

the AI sounds confident even when guessing. if you accept every suggestion without questioning, you'll fix the wrong problem half the time. develop the reflex: "what makes you think so?"

trap 3: "it said it worked"

not verification. verification means YOU confirmed the result with YOUR eyes on the ACTUAL system. an exit code of 0 means the process didn't crash — not that it did the right thing.

trap 4: re-explaining everything

if you're saying the same things every conversation, you're wasting time. write it into a rules file or skill document once. let the AI read it automatically every time.

trap 5: micromanaging code

you don't need to specify which React hooks to use. describe the outcome, let the AI choose the implementation. intervene at decision points — architecture, tradeoffs, business rules — not at every line.

trap 6: trying to predict everything upfront

you can't spec every edge case before building. build the core, test it, discover what's missing, iterate. "what about users who dismiss the modal?" is a question you'll ask after seeing the modal, not before.

mental model

the pottery wheel

you don't design a perfect vase on paper and then execute. you throw clay, shape it, see how it looks, adjust. the first pass gives you the form. iterations give you the art. software works the same way — build the core, then refine based on what you actually see.

check your understanding
you've asked the AI to deploy a change. it responds "deployment complete, all services healthy." what should you NOT do?
never take "deployment complete" at face value without personal verification. load the page, test the feature, check the data. the other three options are all good verification steps.
chapter 8
the investment that compounds
why the scaffolding matters more than the code

here's the counterintuitive truth: the time spent writing documentation for the AI pays back more than the time spent writing code.

a 30-minute skill document saves 15 minutes per conversation. after 20 conversations, that's 5 hours saved. after 100, it's 25 hours. and the quality goes up too — the AI makes fewer mistakes when it has complete context.

mental model

compound interest

your skills and rules files are like a savings account that earns compound interest. every investment (a new skill, a corrected rule, a documented gotcha) makes every future conversation faster and more reliable. teams that invest early have dramatically higher "AI ROI" six months in than teams that keep starting from scratch.

what to build first

  1. a rules file — your non-negotiable standards (commit habits, logging, safety rails). 15 minutes to write. used every conversation.
  2. an infrastructure skill — server addresses, SSH commands, deployment steps, health checks. the most-used skill for any production team.
  3. memory entries — who you are, what you're working on, gotchas you've discovered. builds automatically as you work.
  4. process skills — complex multi-step workflows that would take 20 minutes to re-explain each time.

the meta-lesson

skills are living documentation that happens to be executable. the rules file is a team agreement that happens to be enforced by AI. memory is institutional knowledge that happens to persist across sessions.

the teams that write the best skills debug the fastest, ship the most reliably, and onboard new collaborators — human or AI — with the least friction.

your action items

  • write a rules file with your top 5 non-negotiable standards
  • document your most common workflow as a skill
  • next time you re-explain something, save it to memory instead
  • describe outcomes, not implementations
  • always verify the actual output — never trust "it worked"
  • push back when the AI sounds confident but might be guessing
  • front-load requirements as bullet lists before the AI starts building
check your understanding
what's the highest-leverage thing you can do to improve your AI collaboration over time?
the scaffolding (rules, skills, memory) is what separates teams that get exponentially more productive from teams that reset every conversation. it's compound interest for AI collaboration — every investment makes every future session faster and more reliable.
